 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:  
 
The Sentinel Framework is a vision for quantum engineers dealing with the cusp of 
algorithms and hardware. From cold atom gravimeters to unclonable hardware 
fingerprints. So, our critical infrastructure is protected by gravity locked security 
nodes and hardware root of trust – built by our own engineers, on our own terms.  
The world stands at the threshold of the quantum era. The Q -Day is not a distant 
phenomenon; it is here. Nations such as the US, China, and the EU are investing 
billions to stay ahead in the quantum leap. Quantum hardware, cryptography, and 
sensing are mov ing decisively beyond theoretical simulation toward physical 
sovereignty.  
Pakistan is not exceptional to this transition. We can either import quantum talent 
or import hardware, or we can develop our own workforce capable of designing, 
building, and securing quantum systems.  
In response to the National Center for Quantum Computing (NCQC) call for 
curriculum ideas, this proposal The Sentinel Framewor k is a specialized track for 
the BS in Quantum Computing (BSQC) and the MS in Quantum Systems Engineering 
(MS -QSE).  
The very idea for the Sentinel Framework comes from validated work: the RAQT 
protocol tested on IBM and Chinese quantum hardware  and the “Quantum for All” 
4-tier curriculum (IBM QAMP 2025) . 
The focus on software and hardware interfacing is the new approach adopted here , 
infrastructure resilience and robust capabilities , considering that the physical layer 
is where sovereignty begins. This is where our engineers  can leap ahead.  
 
The Sentinel Mandate is a three -part roadmap:  
(1) integrate PUF, metrology, and secure network electives into the BSQC program;  
(2) establish two specialized labs (MOT/UHV for sensing, AWG/cryo for PUF);  
(3) train faculty within 18 months.  
 
This roadmap aligns with NCQC’s goal of producing industry -ready quantum 
engineers.

 2. Existing Validation: Proven Foundation  
 
The Sentinel Framework is built on real work that I have already done.   
 
 
RAQT Protocol – Tested on IBM and a Chinese Quantum Computer  and NetSquid 
Simulation  
 
RAQT stands for Robust Anonymous Quantum Transmission. It is my implementation of 
the Christandl -Wehner 2004 protocol. The goal is simple: send one secret bit across a 
quantum network without revealing who sent it. You use GHZ states, Z -gate encoding, 
and X-basis measurements. Then you XOR the results. That gives you the bit.  
I coded RAQT from scratch. First in Qiskit, then in NetSquid. Then I took it to real 
hardware.  
• IBM Quantum  – the protocol worked. Ideal simulation gave 100% success. With 
1% depolarising noise, it still succeeded 97% of the time.  
• Origin Wuyuan  – that is a quantum computer in China. I ran the same protocol 
there. It worked too. Cross -platform validation.  
The code, the simulation scripts, and the noise analysis are all on my GitHub. This work 
directly feeds into the  Secure Quantum Networks  elective I am proposing. It also 
proves that our students can go beyond simulators and run code on real machines , 
even on hardware from different countries.  
 
“Quantum for All” Curriculum – IBM QAMP 2025  
“Quantum for All” is a 4 -tier quantum curriculum. I proposed it and led the project as 
part of IBM QAMP (Qiskit Advocate Mentorship Program) 2025.  
Here are the tiers:  
• Tier 1 (grades 6 -8): stories, games, intuition. No  hard  math. Just curiosity.  
• Tier 2 (grades 9 -11):  Bloch sphere, basic probability, visualisations.  
• Tier 3 (undergraduate):  Qiskit programming, formal notation, simple 
algorithms.  
• Tier 4 (expert):  advanced protocols – no-cloning, EPR pairs, QKD, error 
mitigation.

3. BS in Quantum Computing (BSQC): Core Curriculum  
 
Program Narrative & Career Pathways  
The BS in Quantum Computing at NCQC is a four -year degree (130 credit hours) that 
trains engineers to understand quantum systems from the ground up – from the 
mathematical foundations of Hilbert spaces to the physical implementation of qubits 
on real hardware. The programme integrates AI/ML throughout , recognising that the 
coming decade will demand graduates who can both use AI to control quantum 
hardware and develop quantum algorithms to accelerate AI.  
Global benchmarks inform this design.   
Tsinghua University’s “Yao Class” founded by Turing Award winner Andrew Chi -Chih 
Yao has, since 2005, nurtured undergraduates to compete with students from MIT, 
Stanford and Princeton.  
It offers three specialised tracks (Computer Science, Artificial Intelligence, and 
Quantum Information) with a tiered model: the first two years focus on intensive 
foundational training, the final two years on deep specialisation.    
A comparable structure is found at the University of Science and Technology of China 
(USTC), whose quantum information science programme combines physics 
fundamentals (Quantum Mechanics I, Solid State Physics) with specialised 
professional courses in quant um communication, quantum computation and quantum 
precision measurement.   
In the United States, Harvard’s Quantum Science & Engineering PhD programme sits at 
the interface of physics, chemistry and engineering, providing students with 
opportunities to explore the realisation and application of fully engineered quantum 
systems.    
MIT offers a sequence of graduate -level quantum information science courses that 
cover density -matrix formalism, operator -sum representations, fault -tolerance and 
quantum complexity theory.   UC Berkeley now even offers a course in which students 
design and test quantum chips – a first -of-its-kind hands -on hardware experience.

 4. MS in Quantum Systems Engineering (MS -QSE):  
     Advanced Infrastructure  
The MS in Quantum Systems Engineering (MS -QSE) is a two -year (30 -credit) programme 
designed for students who already have a BS in physics, computer science, electrical 
engineering, or a related field. The goal is to move graduates from pure simulation 
to hands -on hardware interfacing, infrastructure design, and security engineering . The 
curriculum is built around two tiers: a computational foundation that leverages HPC 
and the NCQC laboratory, and two strategic research nodes , Atomic Metrology & 
Gravimetry, and Hardware Characterisation & Root of Trust.  
 
The Transition from Simulation to Hardware Interfacing : 
A real quantum engineer must be able to connect a cryostat, tune a laser, or extract a 
PUF response from a raw chip. The MS -QSE closes that gap.  
The programme recognises that  Pakistan cannot import every quantum technician . We 
must train our own people to maintain, calibrate, and secure quantum hardware. The 
transition is organised in three phases:

 Each phase includes a lab component that uses real equipment where available, and 
high -fidelity simulation where hardware is not yet deployed. This mirrors the approach 
of leading programmes such as the  MIT Quantum Engineering  track, the  QuTech 
MSc  (Delft), and the  ETH Zurich Quantum Engineering  master.  
 
Tier 1: Computational Foundations – HPC & NCQC Laboratory  
The first tier provides students with the computational infrastructure needed to design 
and test quantum protocols at scale. It is built around two facilities:  
A. High -Performance Computing (HPC) Cluster  
• Purpose:  Run large -scale simulations of quantum circuits, noise models, and 
quantum networks (up to 30 –40 qubits using state -vector simulators; many 
more using tensor -network methods).  
• Infrastructure:  Existing HPC nodes at UET Lahore/Narowal, augmented with 
GPU accelerators (NVIDIA A100 or equivalent) for machine -learning -assisted 
quantum simulations.  
• Software stack:  Qiskit, Cirq, NetSquid, PennyLane, and custom Python libraries 
for AI -driven error mitigation.  
B. NCQC Quantum Computing Laboratory  
• Purpose:  Provide cloud access to real quantum hardware (IBM Quantum, Origin 
Wuyuan, and future local testbeds) and a local emulation environment.  
• Infrastructure:  Dedicated workstations with Jupyter hubs, version control (Git), 
and a shared repository of verified quantum circuits.  
• Learning outcomes:  Students learn to submit jobs to remote quantum 
processors, analyse measurement results, and account for noise in real 
hardware.

Tier 2: Strategic Research Nodes  
The second tier consists of two specialised research nodes, each equipped with 
simulation tools and (in future) physical hardware. Students choose one node as their 
primary specialisation.  
 
A. Atomic Metrology & Gravimetry Lab (MOT/UHV)  
Mission:  Train engineers to build and operate cold -atom systems for precision sensing 
– gravimeters, gyroscopes, and magnetometers. These are the building blocks of 
gravity -locked navigation (GPS -denied environments).  
Infrastructure (planned):  
• Magneto -Optical Trap (MOT) kits (e.g., from ColdQuanta, TOPTICA, or 
home -built).  
• Ultra -High Vacuum (UHV) chamber with ion pump and titanium sublimation 
pump.  
• Tunable diode lasers (780 nm for rubidium, or 589 nm for sodium).  
• CCD camera and photodetectors for fluorescence imaging.  
• Control electronics (PID controllers, arbitrary waveform generators).  
Learning outcomes:  
• Design and align a MOT (simulated initially, then on a table -top setup).  
• Measure the temperature of trapped atoms via time -of-flight imaging.
